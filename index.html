Data notes

Anomalies & methodological flags

Years where the data requires contextual interpretation before use in trend analysis.

1987

small Einsatz

Only 400 officers deployed — the smallest Einsatz on record. Injured/officer ratio (612/1,000) is an extreme outlier driven by the tiny denominator, not exceptional violence.

1989

small Einsatz

1,600 officers, 346 injured → 216 injured per 1,000. Likely reflects policing capacity limitations in the year the Berlin Wall fell, not a structural peak in crowd violence.

1995

missing data

Einsatz figure missing from available records. Imputed with the series median (5,500) for ratio calculations; excluded from Einsatz trend charts where noted.

2001

endogeneity

A demonstration ban was in effect, suppressing turnout to ~7,750 while simultaneously generating record arrests (616). Normalising arrests by turnout gives a misleadingly extreme ratio — the denominator was legally constrained, not organically low.

2005–2006

structural break

Turnout collapses to 500 (2005) and 50 (2006) — almost certainly reflecting a reorganisation of event format and march routes, including the emergence of Myfest in Kreuzberg as a festival alternative. Not a genuine mobilisation failure.

2009

violence peak

Absolute peak in injured officers (479). Occurs against a background of above-average but not exceptional turnout (8,800). The spike is real and robust across all index variants; no methodological artefact explains it.

2020

COVID-19

COVID-19 containment regulations banned mass gatherings. Turnout (3,000) reflects the legal cap, not voluntary mobilisation. Arrests (350) were predominantly for violating the Eindämmungsmaßnahmenverordnung, not protest-related offences — a categorically different arrest type.

Structural interpretation

Three structural periods

1987–2004

High, variable violence

The classic Berlin model: high and volatile RDI, stable turnout of 10–15k. Kreuzberg SO36 as the primary flashpoint. Violence driven by a core of autonomous left groups.

2005–2010

Turbulence & peak

Turnout disrupted by format reorganisation (2005–06), then recovering. Violence reaches its absolute peak in 2009 (479 injured). Competing dynamics: Myfest pacification vs. continued autonomous confrontations.

2011–2026

The scissors effect

Turnout grows systematically to record highs. Violence falls to historic lows. The dilution hypothesis: radical participants become a smaller share of a larger crowd. Institutionalisation of May Day as a festival likely plays a reinforcing role.
